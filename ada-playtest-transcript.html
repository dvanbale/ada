Designer Notes — Mechanics per the Published Handbook

High Concept is always rolled. The earlier draft had Trouble replace the High Concept die. The published handbook is explicit that Knack and Trouble only ever add to High Concept — they never replace it. This shows up most clearly in Scenes 3 and 7: Trouble piles 2 control dice on top of a pool that still includes HC, rather than swapping anything out. It's a small change in wording but a real change in the math — every Trouble-driven roll in this transcript has one more player die in it than the earlier version did.

CR is a fact about the task, not about the character attempting it. An earlier pass set Cipher's camera-loop CR at 3 instead of 4, reasoning that his Knack made the task easier for him specifically. That's backwards — the "could an average person do this" test is supposed to produce the same CR regardless of who's rolling. Competence lives entirely in the dice pool (HC + Knack + Skill + Approach); the CR should never flex downward because the GM knows the character is good at this. Scene 5's ambush roll models the fix directly — Ghost's surprise and combat training show up as a fat six-die pool, while the CR is set purely on "could Bob survive a fight with an armed, trained guard." That's the pattern to repeat: build the case for the CR as if the GM doesn't know who's about to roll.

A second pass on the same mistake, in the opposite direction. Combat against a trained, armed guard was originally set at CR 3 (Standard) twice in this transcript — once for Ghost's ambush, once for sneaking past the second guard. Running both through Bob-from-Accounting cleanly: would Bob survive a fight with an armed, trained guard, or slip past one on alert? No, not even a little. A clear "no" for an average person belongs at CR 4 or 5, the same tier as the tower climb — not CR 3, which should be reserved for tasks an average person could plausibly scrape through with effort. Both rolls are now CR 4 in this version, with the dice rebalanced to land on the same outcomes. The corrected rule of thumb: if the honest answer to "could Bob do this" is an unambiguous no, the CR has to say so — 3 is for genuinely contestable tasks, not a default setting for "fight" or "sneak."

NPC Consequences come off the CR, not a player-die pool that doesn't exist. An earlier draft described Guard Alpha's "Dislocated Shoulder" as subtracting a player die from his contribution — but NPCs never roll player dice in the first place; their entire presence in a scene is the control dice their CR contributes. A Consequence on an NPC has to lower that CR by its rating instead, the mirror image of how a PC Consequence subtracts a player die. Worth stating directly in the handbook's NPC chapter, since the parallel isn't obvious until you try to write the wrong version down.

Create Advantage no longer exists as its own action. What used to be a standalone move is now just the natural consequence of any Overcome roll: hits beyond the CR bank as an Advantage die, full stop. Cipher's camera loop in Scene 2 and Ghost's corridor slip in Scene 3 both demonstrate this — neither needed a special action type, just hits to spare on an ordinary roll. This is a cleaner system than having a fourth action category to remember.

Advantage dice are a shared table resource. Scene 8 leans on this directly — Ghost banks 2 white dice from her Scene 3 success, then spends one on her own escape roll and hands the other to Sable for hers. The handbook's wording ("available to anyone at the table who can plausibly use it") makes this kind of cross-character spending legitimate, and it's a satisfying payoff for banking hits earlier instead of cashing them in immediately.

Drive only triggers on a Straight Success. An earlier draft of this transcript mistakenly tied Drive gains to rolling a 10. The handbook ties it strictly to the outcome, not the dice that produced it — Ghost's Scene 1 success earned Drive despite no exploding dice at all, while several exploding 10s elsewhere produced no Drive when the outcome landed as a Cost or Failure instead.

Control tokens now explicitly trigger on both Success at a Cost and Critical Failure.
